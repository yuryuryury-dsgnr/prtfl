03 — UX Strategy

Two phases of product evolution

UX strategy is a long-term plan that connects user insights, product vision, and business objectives into a coherent direction for designing and improving the user experience.

Phase 1 established the foundation — a single marketplace where engineers can find, trust, and access any data asset. The focus was on structure, clarity, and core workflows before layering in AI and social.

Vision & Problem
VisionProvide a user-friendly means of accessing curated, trusted data, reports, models, and AI Agents that are a part of Halliburton's Data Ribbon.
ProblemUsers cannot efficiently work with data because it is difficult to find, difficult to access, and not easy to compare across multiple sources.
Success looks likeData engineers use the dashboard daily as the primary entry point to find, validate, request access, and consume data assets within minutes — not days or weeks.
Persona groups
G1

Data Scientist / Engineer

Browse data products, fast and relevant search, preview data, request & obtain access quickly.

G2

Business User

Find existing reports across BI tools that answer questions from trusted data.

G3

Data Product Owner

Manage data product info, approve/reject accesses, define level of access.

G4

Data POD Lead

Oversee data products across teams, monitor quality and access patterns.

UX Goals
  • Help users find the right data product quickly
  • Make data product cards and details easy to understand
  • Simplify the access request flow
  • Make access statuses and permissions clear
  • Differentiate View and Edit access levels clearly
  • Enable product owners to add and manage products with minimal friction
Core user scenarios
  • Search and discover relevant data products
  • Filter and narrow down data products
  • Open dataset detail page and understand if it's usable
  • Request and get 1-click access directly from dataset page
  • Track access request status
  • Compare data sets
  • Add and manage data products
  • Manage access levels
UX Principles
  • Reduce cognitive load — engineers already drown in data; the UI shouldn't add noise.
  • Trust over decoration — every element earns its place; metadata, status, and lineage come first.
  • Speed as a feature — every flow optimised for “minutes, not days.”
  • Desktop-first — users live in multi-window data environments.

05 — Prototype & Design System

From AI brainstorm to production-ready screens

Instead of jumping straight into pixel-perfect mockups, I started with an AI-based prototype to quickly test concepts, layouts, and interaction patterns. This let me brainstorm and validate what works and what doesn't — before investing time in high-fidelity design.

Once the structure was proven, I built a scalable design system with reusable components, tokens, and patterns — then created the final mockups for the dev team.

AI prototype approach

Used AI tools to rapidly generate and iterate on layout concepts, testing different approaches to search, filtering, data cards, and access flows. This phase was about speed and exploration — finding the right direction before committing to a design system.

Prompt

Generate a data marketplace homepage layout with: a prominent search bar at the top for natural language queries, a left sidebar with category filters (data type, domain, certification status), and a main grid of data product cards showing name, owner, quality score, and access status. Use a clean editorial style with generous whitespace. Focus on information density without clutter — engineers should scan 20+ items without fatigue.

Multiple iterations across layout, search, filtering, and access flow concepts — tested structurally before any high-fidelity work began.

06 — Validation

Direct feedback from real users

Before moving to development, I ran two structured feedback sessions with the primary user groups — validating designs, aligning on roles and workflows, and catching blind spots early.

Session 1 — Data POD LeadsNDA

Design review & feedback session with Data POD Leads — the technical owners who create and maintain data products in Databricks. Focused on validating roles, publishing workflows, and access management.

RolesValidated 4 user groups: Data Engineers, Business Users, Data Product Owners (DPO), and Data POD Leads. Confirmed separation between technical ownership (POD Lead) and business ownership (DPO) as critical.
PublishingAgreed on two models: manual review or automated pipeline. In automation, products auto-appear as "Ready for review" — DPO decides when to publish to end users.
Editing scopeData Product Owners do not edit schemas or tables. DPO edits: thumbnail, image, description, tags. All technical validation happens upstream in Databricks.
Data previewMarketplace shows read-only previews pulled from Databricks: schema, sample data, technical metadata. Positioned as a presentation layer, not a source of truth.
Access managementThree access levels: Owner, Editor, Viewer. Groups managed through AD integration. Users must understand AD to manage access properly.
SensitivitySensitivity treated as a UI-level attribute — no column-level or row-level sensitivity in scope. Managing access at the product level only.
User contextAccess requests must include: Name, Role, Team/POD, Domain, Requested level, Environment, and Justification — so owners can make informed decisions.
Protected under NDA

Key takeaways

  • Overall Marketplace concept is supported
  • Clear separation between POD Lead and DPO responsibilities
  • Access management should be user-centric
  • Sensitivity column removed from UI
  • Dev access management remains an open exploration topic
Session 2 — Data Product OwnersNDA

Feedback & alignment session with Data Product Owners — the business-side stakeholders responsible for publication decisions and access governance.

Role alignmentDPOs confirmed their role as business owners of data products — deciding when a product is ready for consumption and appropriate for broader visibility.
Publication flowProducts are created and validated by POD Leads, then become available for review. DPOs perform final business review and publish to the marketplace.
Review scopeDPO review focuses on business readiness: product description, naming, intended usage, and audience. Technical validation handled upstream by POD Leads.
Access approvalsDPOs expect clear user context to support informed decisions. Permission handling remains abstracted — focusing on who is requesting access and for what purpose.
Landing pageFirst impression matters: needs to clearly show what data products exist. Tagging should be useful and well-structured.
Data detail pageAdd tabs to switch between Description and Sample Data for easier preview. Edit options for Name, Description, Tags, or schema view.
Protected under NDA

Key takeaways

  • Overall Marketplace concept is supported by DPOs
  • Clear separation between technical and business ownership is essential
  • Access management should be user-centric with clear "access reason"
  • Publication and access decisions should remain lightweight and efficient
  • Marketplace should enable confident decisions without exposing technical complexity

07 — Hand-off to Dev

AI-powered design-to-code pipeline

Instead of traditional hand-off with static specs and redlines, we connected the dev team directly to the design system through an MCP (Model Context Protocol) server linked to Figma.

This meant developers could query the design system programmatically — pulling component specs, tokens, spacing values, and interaction states directly into their code environment. No more guessing from screenshots or outdated documentation.

How MCP + Figma worked
ConnectionMCP server connected to Figma file, exposing the design system as structured data that dev tools could consume in real time.
ComponentsDevelopers accessed component specs, props, variants, and usage guidelines directly from their IDE — no context switching to Figma.
TokensDesign tokens (colors, typography, spacing, elevation) synced automatically — any update in Figma propagated to code.
ResultSignificantly reduced back-and-forth between design and dev. Components were built accurately on the first pass, matching the design system 1:1.
What made this successful
  • Design system was structured with clear naming conventions and consistent component architecture
  • Every component had documented states, variants, and responsive behavior
  • MCP eliminated the "lost in translation" problem — devs worked with the source of truth, not interpretations
  • Reduced QA cycles: fewer visual bugs, fewer "this doesn't match the design" tickets
  • Team could focus on logic and performance instead of pixel-pushing

08 — Post-launch Research

Measuring impact and iterating

Launch wasn't the finish line. After the platform went live, I ran a structured research program to track real usage, validate earlier assumptions, and identify areas for continuous improvement.

What we measured
AdoptionTracked active users, onboarding completion rates, and frequency of return visits across all persona groups.
Task successMeasured time-to-find for datasets, search-to-access conversion, and first-attempt success rate for access requests.
SatisfactionRan post-launch CSAT surveys and collected qualitative feedback through in-app prompts and follow-up interviews.
Pain pointsIdentified remaining friction through session recordings, support tickets, and heatmap analysis on key pages.
Key findings & iterationsNDA
SearchUsers found datasets faster than expected, but filter combinations needed refinement — added saved filters and recent searches.
Trust signalsQuality badges and certification status significantly increased click-through — expanded to all data product types.
Access flowApproval bottleneck identified: owners didn't respond fast enough. Introduced auto-escalation and SLA reminders.
OnboardingNew users struggled with terminology. Added contextual tooltips and a guided first-visit experience.
MobileUnexpected mobile usage from field engineers. Prioritized responsive improvements for key read-only flows.
Protected under NDA
Continuous improvement process
  • Bi-weekly review of analytics dashboards with product and engineering leads
  • Monthly usability pulse checks — 5-minute unmoderated tests on new features
  • Quarterly deep-dive research reports with prioritized recommendations
  • Design system updates based on real component usage data and dev feedback
  • Backlog grooming informed by research insights, not assumptions

10 — Reflection

Looking back

What I'd do differently

Involve data governance stakeholders earlier in the process. Their requirements surfaced late and required reworking the access request flow mid-project.

What I learned

Enterprise data products need trust-building at every layer — from metadata quality to access transparency. Users won't adopt a tool if they can't trust the data inside it.
